Critique of Interactive Data Visualization:

Kontinentalist's Terraforming Singapore

Assignment 2 for the course 02.526 Interactive Data Visualization
of the Singapore University of Technology and Design
Presented by Cherie Lynne Gomintong on 20 March 2025

→

This assignment critiques some data visualizations from Terraforming Singapore: Is the future made of sand? by Nabilah Said from the Kontinentalist. Disclaimer: These are not terrible visualizations.

In fact, this is one of my favorite storytelling data visualizations with interactive features, so it was personally challenging to critique. For this assignment, I pick two visualizations from this online magazine and discuss some points of improvement from a user's perspective.

"Reclamation project": When these "x" markers are clicked, they're not highlighted. I can easily mistake the project description for other markers shown in the map.

Suggestion: When selected, change marker color from white to another color, such as bright yellow. This will also help the user confirm that the click was successful.
Legend for 1957 to 2023: Good and intuitive color gradation. The darker the color, the longer that piece of land has been there.
"Future": Poor choice of color. It easily blends in with the background, i.e. with the seas and the non-Singapore pieces of land.

Suggestion: Choose a color that will still not be aesthetically disruptive but will have better contrast with the background.
"Previous": Why is this here? Unnecessary placement. I accidently clicked it and was transferred to another online magazine on Cambodia's monkey farms. It isn't very visible here, but there's a "Next" button at the rightmost of the window, too.

Suggestion: Omit. Link other stories at the bottom of the page or at the end of the online magazine. This is to prevent accidental interruption of the storytelling.

Legends are not proportionate: My intuition was to take the areas of these circles to be proportionate with the country land area. When I noticed that Singapore is almost the same size as Germany, I had the urge to verify their size difference and it turns out Germany is almost 500 times larger than Singapore. The urge to double check, to me, indicates doubt.

Suggestion: The chart is still able to convey the relatively stark amounts of sand Singapore has imported, so I think it still works. A disclaimer that the legends are not to scale will help in easing user distrust though.
No details when hovering over the data: Related to the urge to manually verify the country land area, having the ability to hover and see the amount of sand and country land area of the data points will help build user trust.

Suggestion: Show data values when hovering over a data point.
"Other Asia" not defined: I only have a slight idea on what "Other Asia" in this chart could mean. It adds cognitive load to the user to try to understand undefined elements in the chart.

Suggestion: Add a footnote explaining what "Other Asia" is composed of.
